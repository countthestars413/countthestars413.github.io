The Confederation Creek watershed was historically composed of many ephemeral and perennial streams fed by runoff and groundwater emerging from porous Porcupine Hills sandstone. Movement patterns were defined on the interrelations of geology, hydrology, and ecology.

Suburban development culverted streams and greatly reduced greenspace. A priority on vehicular traffic flow broke ecological connectivity and degraded public realm quality for pedestrians and cyclists. Water no longer defines the landscape in a mutual interaction of weathering and flow. Instead, paths are forged by the needs of sprawl.

Freeing the flow of water has ripple effects on other journeys.

This design proposes the re-balancing of human and natural systems, envisioning a future of connection, resilience, and equity between people, ecosystems, and natural processes.

The three main interventions include:

Daylighting perennial streams and building greenways where major vehicle-exclusive highways are now located

Creating a wetland complex for biodiversity, discovery, and bio-filtration

Establishing a public natural swimming pool for joyful resilience
